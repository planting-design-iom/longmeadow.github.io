Ecological Management Planning

An Ecological Management Plan outlines the strategies and measures required to manage and/or mitigate ecological impacts associated with a project, activity or organisation. Management plans are common for large‑scale development, industrial and infrastructure projects, but are also useful for smaller land‑use changes such as creating places for nature or setting out how to meet conservation aims through long‑term site management.

Typical components include:

  • Objectives & scope – clear goals and boundaries for the plan.
  • Legal & regulatory context – Manx legislation, permits and approvals that apply.
  • Baseline conditions – existing habitats, flora and fauna, soils and water.
  • Potential impacts – direct and indirect effects associated with proposals.
  • Mitigation measures – procedures and habitat creation/restoration to minimise impacts.
  • Stakeholder engagement – how communities and authorities are involved.
  • Review & update – periodic checks to reflect changes in scope, regulation or conditions.

Preliminary Ecological Assessment (PEA)

A PEA aims to identify ecological constraints to a proposed development and opportunities for habitat enhancement. If priority habitats or protected species are likely (e.g. bats, amphibians, reptiles or notable plants), we will set out the additional surveys required.

The process typically includes:

  • Desk study – gathering existing records from local sources such as Manx Wildlife Trust, IOM Recorder and natural history groups, plus relevant UK sources where helpful.
  • Field study – an extended Phase 1 Habitat Survey with a site walkover to record habitats and any evidence of protected or notable species.
  • Evaluation & reporting – a clear report describing methods, results, likely impacts, recommended mitigation/enhancement and any further surveys needed to meet planning policy and legislation.

For many sites a PEA provides sufficient ecological information to accompany a planning application; where further work is needed, the PEA will specify it.

Ecological Impact Assessment (EIA)

Often following a PEA, an EIA evaluates the likely ecological impacts of a proposed development and identifies how biodiversity loss can be avoided or reduced.

  • Scoping & data gathering – understanding the proposal, site ecology and applicable legislation/guidance.
  • Surveys – targeted protected‑species and habitat surveys where required.
  • Evaluation – the site’s conservation value and the importance of habitats and species (from negligible to international).
  • Impact assessment – interpreting survey data to identify habitat loss/change, its magnitude and significance.
  • Mitigation & compensation – measures to avoid, reduce or offset impacts, including on‑ or off‑site biodiversity gains.
  • Residual effects – assessing outcomes after mitigation.
  • Reporting – a concise EIA report suitable for stakeholders and planning.

About us

Long Meadow Ecological Consultants & Landscape Design is a Manx business with local knowledge of the Island’s environment, legislation and laws. Our expertise comes from many years working for both the Manx Government and on private‑sector projects. Our strength is listening carefully to clients, advising where necessary and delivering work in a timely, concise and professional manner.

Our clients range from larger organisations seeking change of use, wildlife‑positive site management or nature‑rich grounds (pollinator‑friendly planting, ponds and spaces to unwind), to individuals submitting planning applications who need ecological or environmental assessments. We can advise on what is needed, liaise with the Planning Department on your behalf, and undertake survey work and report writing to meet application requirements.

Our landscaping design services are client‑led. We take your ideas and incorporate nature‑friendly planting and design to create spaces where people and wildlife coexist in harmony.
